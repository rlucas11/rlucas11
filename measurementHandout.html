As we would expect, the correlations are higher when we look between persons than within (because there is more measurement error in the activity-level data). There is a relatively clear tendency for same-valence emotion items to correlate more strongly than opposite-valence items; this is true at both levels. Correlations between emotion items and the tired and pain items tend to be lower (suggesting that they may hang together with the emotions).

I used Mplus to run a multilevel exploratory factor analysis, first using all seven items and then using just the five emotion items. Scree plots for the first analyses are reported below, separately for the between- and within-person levels. Consistent with the correlations, there is a stronger first factor for the between-person data.

The results are somewhat ambiguous when all seven items are included. There is a strong first factor, and two factors that pass the eigen-value-greater-than-one threshold. However, it is less clear whether there is a break after one or three factors.

Scree Plot for All Seven Ratings

Importantly, when I tried to extract and rotate one to three factors, there were often estimation problems, especially when extracting two between-person factors. When looking at results just for the emotion items, things get a little clearer, with a clear single-factor dominating:

Scree Plot for Five Affect Ratings

We can look at the reliability of the scale scores. The Cronbachs alpha for all five items at the between person level is 0.91. At the within-person level, the alpha is 0.71. For just the positive affect scale, the between- and within-person alphas are:0.83 and 0.62. For the negative affect scale, the between- and within-person alphas are: 0.93 and 0.65. Finally, for the two somatic items, the between- and within-person alphas are: 0.74 and 0.42.

We can also calculate alphas for each scale within an activity. The average alphas for the total score, positive affect, negative affect, and somatic scales are: 0.78, 0.58, 0.72, and 0.54 , respectively.

Another way to think about reliability is how stable these are over short periods of time (e.g., from the weekend report to the weekday). So I aggregated across the three activities within each day and calculated stability of the scale scores, which are reported below.

Stabilities are only moderate, even for the constructed scale scores. This may mean that (a) three activities does not lead to a reliable estimate of daily experiential well-being or (b) there is reliable day-level variance that changes from day to day. Our prior work on the year-to-year stability actually found higher levels of stability even over a year (Hudson, Lucas, and Donnellan 2016Hudson, Nathan W., Richard E. Lucas, and M. Brent Donnellan. 2016. Day-to-Day Affect Is Surprisingly Stable: A Two-Year Longitudinal Study of Well-Being. Social Psychological and Personality Science.).

Issues

  • Weight by length of activity or number of days when creating person-level scale scores?
  • Use any criterion variables as tests of validity? The ATUS study used health, but we could also use life satisfaction, personality, etc. We could also look at these predictors within and between persons.
  • Any better arguments for one versus two factors? How should we explain the difficulties we encountered when conducting the EFA?
  • Is this enough?
